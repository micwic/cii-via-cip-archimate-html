### **CII via CIP Architecture Vision Data Object (TOGAF-Aligned)** The **CII via CIP Architecture Vision** data object, aligned with TOGAF’s **Phase A: Architecture Vision**, represents a high-level and conceptual understanding of the desired architecture. It includes an initial consideration of organizational capabilities to provide context for future design and implementation decisions without delving deeply into detailed analysis. --- ### **Contents** 1. **Vision Statement** - A high-level declaration of the intended purpose and outcomes of the architecture. - Example: "Enable secure, real-time access to energy consumption data via smart meters, aligning with regulatory, operational, and customer-focused goals." 2. **Objectives** - Define the key outcomes the architecture aims to achieve, such as: - Regulatory compliance (e.g., Swiss LApEl). - Improved customer access to data. - Integration with existing energy management systems. 3. **Guiding Principles** - Broad principles to guide the architectural process: - Modularity for future flexibility. - Scalability to handle increasing data and user demands. - Security to ensure data integrity and confidentiality. 4. **Capability Considerations** - Acknowledge existing organizational capabilities and the general readiness to implement the envisioned architecture. - Current strengths: Existing smart meter infrastructure, basic customer data access portals. - Preliminary gaps: Limited scalability and integration capabilities, evolving compliance requirements. - These considerations provide context for understanding high-level challenges and opportunities. 5. **Scope** - Broadly define the areas the architecture will address: - Functional: Smart meter integration, data visualization, customer portals. - Technical: System interoperability, secure data exchange. - Regulatory: Adherence to IDIS and DSMR P1 standards. 6. **Stakeholder Alignment** - Identification of primary stakeholders and their expectations: - **Business Owner:** Ensure the solution aligns with strategic goals. - **Technical Teams:** Ensure architectural feasibility. - **Regulators:** Address legal and compliance needs. 7. **Constraints and Assumptions** - Outline known constraints (e.g., budget, timelines) and assumptions (e.g., availability of certain technologies). --- ### **Role in TOGAF Artifacts** 1. **Architecture Vision Document** - The data object forms part of the Architecture Vision Document, capturing the high-level intent of the architecture. 2. **Capability Considerations in Context** - The capability assessment is integrated subtly as context, rather than a formal analysis, acknowledging organizational readiness to support the architecture. --- ### **Benefits of this Approach** - **Clarity:** Maintains focus on high-level goals without delving into detailed capability assessments. - **Context:** Provides enough consideration of organizational capabilities to inform later phases. - **Flexibility:** Avoids premature specificity, allowing room for iterative development.
